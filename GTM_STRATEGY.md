# GTM Strategy & Commercial Rationale

How and why the system prioritizes, sequences, and scales outreach across
the two customer types. Every number here is real - generated by actually
running the commands in this repo, not estimated. Re-run any command below
to reproduce them.

---

## Who gets today's 40 Instagram DMs, and why

### The mechanism, in full

Every Instagram-channel lead gets sorted by two layers, computed in
`src/scoring.py`:

**Layer 1 - Tier (a hard gate, computed from `stage`):**

| Tier | Stages | Cooldown | Score base |
|---|---|---|---|
| `waiting_on_us` | replied, warm, negotiating, call_booked | none - always eligible | 1000 |
| `new` | new (never contacted) | none - always eligible | 500 |
| `follow_up_due` | contacted, cooldown elapsed | 3 days (Instagram) | 200 |
| `re_engage` | ghosted, cooldown elapsed | 7 days (Instagram) | 50 |
| *(excluded)* | won, lost | - never re-messaged | - |

A lead in `waiting_on_us` **cannot be outscored** by anything in a lower
tier, no matter how big the account is - the gap between tiers (1000 vs
500 vs 200 vs 50) is far larger than anything Layer 2 can add. This is
deliberate, not a tuning accident: the brief itself says *"a lot of these
leads are sitting half-replied with nobody following up... that's most of
the job,"* and B2B lead-scoring research independently confirms that
funnel stage/status is one of the single strongest individual predictors
of conversion. Both point the same direction, so tier is the gate, not
just another weighted input.

**Layer 2 - Fit + Engagement (only breaks ties *within* a tier):**

```
score = TIER_SCORE[tier] + (0.6 * fit_score + 0.4 * engagement_score) * 100

fit_score        = 0.50*spend + 0.25*sales_velocity + 0.15*active_listings + 0.10*followers
engagement_score = 0.50*has_replied + 0.30*touch_count + 0.20*recency
```

This mirrors the standard structure used in real B2B lead scoring (Fit /
Engagement / funnel-stage), not an invented rubric - see `ARCHITECTURE.md`
for why. Spend dominates Fit because it's the most direct £ signal we
have; a real reply dominates Engagement because it's a much stronger
intent signal than just being touched a lot.

### Walking it through with the actual data

Running the real pipeline today (265 handover leads + the 30-lead day-2
drop, 278 total canonical leads after dedup):

```
$ python -m src.cli plan --date 2026-06-25
  Instagram DM queue: 40 / cap 40 (40 new, 0 already queued today)
```

**Every single one of today's 40 slots went to `waiting_on_us` leads** -
not because I forced that, but because there are currently 152
Instagram-channel leads, and enough of them are sitting in `replied` /
`warm` / `negotiating` to fill the entire cap before a single `new` lead
gets a look. That's the real, measured proof that the brief's framing
("getting stalled conversations going again is most of the job") is
exactly what happens once you run this for real - not a hope, an outcome.

Within that 40, ordering is by Fit+Engagement: an account with £9,000/mo
estimated spend, 46,640 followers, and a reply already in hand
(`tonicstitchthreads`, score 1088.4) sits above one with similar
engagement but lower estimated spend - same tier, value breaks the tie.

### The honest limitation

The weights above (0.50/0.25/0.15/0.10 and 0.6/0.4) are a *reasoned*
starting point, not a *proven* one - there are only 9 won / 14 lost leads
in the handover, nowhere near the ~70 needed (10 outcomes per feature,
EPV rule of thumb, 7 features) to fit real weights with confidence. Every
action already logs the score it had at the time, and `python -m src.cli
recalibrate` checks honestly, every time it's run, whether there's
finally enough real outcome data to fit weights properly via logistic
regression - it refuses and says so otherwise. Verified this actually
works by deliberately seeding 300 synthetic outcomes with a known
spend-predicts-winning relationship and confirming it recovered that
relationship correctly (see `DEVELOPMENT_LOG.md`).

## Scaling from 265 to 30,000 leads

### What's actually proven to scale (the code)

| Operation | At 265 leads | At 30,000 leads (synthetic test) |
|---|---|---|
| Ingest (clean + dedupe) | <1s | ~9-13s |
| Scoring | instant | ~0.5-0.6s |
| Full `plan` (scoring + CSV/dashboard) | instant | ~0.6s |
| Repeated daily runs (15-day simulation) | - | flat, no degradation as the action log grows |

The engine itself does not fall over. That's verified, not assumed.

### What does NOT scale automatically (the real constraint)

`python -m src.cli backlog-forecast` exists specifically to surface this
honestly instead of hiding behind "the code handles it":

| | At 265 leads | At 30,000 leads (synthetic) |
|---|---|---|
| Instagram backlog | ~4 days to clear | **~341 days** at the fixed 40/day platform cap |
| Store backlog (email+call+visit) | ~1 day to clear | **~66 days** at today's placeholder caps |

That's the real answer to "how do you get to 30,000 without quality
falling off": **the code was never the bottleneck - team and platform
capacity is.** Specifically:

- **Instagram is capped by the platform itself, not by us.** One account
  can't realistically clear that backlog. Scaling this channel means
  either running multiple warmed Instagram accounts in parallel (each
  with its own 40/day pool and cooldown tracking - the code already
  supports per-account capacity, it just needs the cap parameterized per
  account once that's a real decision Fleek makes) or deliberately
  de-prioritizing Instagram relative to channels that scale better.
- **Email is the channel that genuinely scales with automation** - the
  real ceiling is sender deliverability (a Gmail account caps around
  500/day; a proper ESP goes much higher with warm-up), not headcount.
  This is the channel to lean into hardest as volume grows.
- **Calls and visits stay genuinely human-time-limited** - scaling these
  means hiring, not just running the script more often. `visit-plan`'s
  city-clustering (only book a trip once a city has 3+ ready stores,
  configurable) exists specifically so that human time isn't wasted on
  small, scattered trips as volume grows.
- **`config/assumptions.yaml`** is the literal seam for plugging in real
  numbers once Fleek has them - team headcount, real ESP limits, and
  (via `channel_performance`) real conversion rates per channel, which
  `rebalance-caps` uses to recommend shifting capacity toward whichever
  channel is actually converting best, rather than treating all capacity
  as equally valuable by default.

### What a normal week looks like once it's running

1. **Every morning, unattended:** GitHub Actions runs the full pipeline on
   a schedule (no laptop required) - ingests anything new, scores
   everyone, builds today's capped DM/email/call/visit queues, plans the
   week's city visits, and publishes a live dashboard.
2. **A rep opens the dashboard**, not a terminal - clicks into the 40 DM
   leads (drafted, ready to send), the day's email/call/visit batch, and
   the visit-plan tab to see which cities are worth a trip this week.
3. **They act, then mark it sent** - which is what actually advances the
   lead's cooldown and lets the next-priority lead surface tomorrow.
4. **Periodically** (weekly, say): check `backlog-forecast` to see if the
   queue is falling behind capacity, check `review-queue` for data-quality
   issues that need a human look, and once there's enough real outcome
   data, run `recalibrate` / `rebalance-caps` to let real results start
   correcting the starting assumptions.

## The two channels: different approaches, different AI/human split

| | Online resellers | Physical shops |
|---|---|---|
| **Contact method** | Instagram DM only (unless they happen to have an email - then they're treated as direct-contactable regardless of the source label) | Email -> call -> visit, escalating by touch count |
| **Real constraint** | Platform-enforced 40/day per account | Human time (calls, visits) or sender deliverability (email) |
| **What AI does end-to-end** | Cleans/dedupes the list, decides who's actually a reseller vs a store regardless of label, scores and ranks within the 40/day cap, drafts the DM - and now drafts a reply that actually responds to what they said (objection vs question vs interest), not a generic wrapper | Same cleaning/scoring, decides the email/call/visit sequence per lead, decides which cities are worth a dedicated trip, drafts the email and the call/visit briefing notes |
| **Where a human (or a future agent) has to step in** | Actually sending the DM - Instagram restricts bulk-sending automation, so this is a real point where a person (or a deliberately-chosen, compliant sending tool) has to act, not the model | Email sending could be automated end-to-end (API into Gmail/an ESP); calls and visits cannot - those need an actual human until/unless Fleek makes a separate, deliberate call to use AI voice agents |

The underlying logic is genuinely shared (same scoring, same tier
priority, same idempotency, same dashboard) - what differs is the
sequencing and the constraint each channel hits first.
